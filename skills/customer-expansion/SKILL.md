# Customer Expansion

You closed the deal. Now the real work starts.

Most enterprise revenue is lost not in sales but in the 12 months after the contract is signed.
Champion goes quiet. Usage plateaus. Procurement resets the price at renewal. The $50K pilot
that was supposed to become $500K is still $50K eighteen months later — if it renews at all.

This skill is the third act of the enterprise playbook. `enterprise-account-planning` gets you
to signed. `enterprise-onboarding` gets you through the first 90 days. This gets you to
compounding NRR.

---

## When to Use

- Your NRR is under 120% and you're not sure why
- Expansion conversations happen at renewal instead of 90 days before it
- Your CSMs own the relationship but not the revenue number
- You have a land-and-expand strategy on paper with no triggers, owners, or timelines
- A champion just left and you don't know who's next in the account
- You're about to negotiate a renewal you haven't already pre-closed

## Core Frameworks

### Framework 1: The Expansion Trigger Stack

Expansion doesn't happen because you asked. It happens because something changed — in the
customer's business, in their usage, or in their organization. Most teams wait for a calendar
reminder instead of monitoring for triggers.

**Tier 1 — Usage triggers (respond within 5 business days):**
- Seat utilization crosses 80% for two consecutive weeks
- A team outside the original buyer's org starts using the product
- API call volume exceeds 70% of contracted limit
- A new use case appears in support tickets that isn't in the original contract scope

**Tier 2 — Business triggers (open a conversation within 2 weeks):**
- Company raises a new funding round
- Champion gets promoted or changes role
- Earnings call or press release mentions a strategic priority your product addresses
- A new exec joins in a function you don't have a relationship with

**Tier 3 — Relationship triggers (use for multi-threading, not immediate expansion):**
- Champion introduces you to a peer voluntarily
- Executive sponsor asks to be added to a QBR
- Champion asks for a reference on your behalf

**The mistake:** treating all triggers as sales opportunities. Tier 1 triggers warrant a
proactive value conversation, not a pitch. The pitch comes after you've confirmed the value
is landing.

### Framework 2: The Expansion Conversation Sequence

The reason expansion conversations feel awkward is that teams run them backwards — they start
with the ask instead of the evidence.

**Step 1 — Surface the data (Week 1 of trigger window)**
Pull usage data, business outcomes achieved to date, and any qualitative feedback. Frame
this as a check-in, not a sales call. Send a one-page summary before the meeting.

**Step 2 — Confirm the outcome (Week 2)**
Ask: "Based on what you've seen, where are you getting the most value? Where are you not?"
You are looking for two things: proof that the original use case delivered, and a new problem
they mention that you can solve. If they mention a new problem, don't pitch. Ask questions.

**Step 3 — Map the next use case (Week 3)**
Only after you've confirmed outcome delivery: "We've seen other companies at your stage use
[product] for [adjacent use case]. Is that on your radar?" This is a hypothesis, not a demo
request. Listen for energy.

**Step 4 — Build the internal business case (Weeks 4–6)**
The champion doesn't buy the expansion — their CFO or VP does. Your job is to give the
champion the ammunition to make the case internally. That means ROI framing, risk
mitigation language, and a comparison to the status quo — not a feature list.

**Step 5 — Pre-close before procurement (Week 6–8)**
If you reach procurement without a verbal commitment from the economic buyer, you have
already lost leverage. The expansion should be verbally closed before a PO is ever raised.

### Framework 3: The NRR Math That Precedes the Problem

NRR problems are visible in the revenue line six months after they're visible in the product.
By the time NRR shows up as a miss, the accounts that caused it are already churned or
contracted. You need leading indicators.

**Monthly health score inputs (weight each):**
- Seat utilization rate vs. contracted seats: 30%
- Feature adoption breadth (are they using 1 feature or 5?): 25%
- Time-to-value completion (did they hit the original success metric?): 25%
- Stakeholder depth (how many contacts do you have in the account?): 20%

**Thresholds:**
- Score ≥ 80: expansion-ready, begin trigger monitoring
- Score 60–79: retention risk, escalate to CS leadership, do not open expansion conversation
- Score < 60: churn risk, executive sponsor involvement required within 2 weeks

**The mistake teams make:** a healthy CSAT score with a low health score. Customers will tell
you they love the product right up until they don't renew. Sentiment is not a leading indicator.
Usage and outcome achievement are.

---

### Framework 4: Multi-Threading Before You Need It

Single-threaded accounts are the primary cause of surprise churn. The pattern is always the
same: champion leaves, new person has their own vendor preferences, the relationship doesn't
transfer.

**The three relationships you need in every account:**
1. **Champion** — the person who sold the project internally. They are your voice when you're
   not in the room.
2. **Economic buyer** — the person who controls the budget. You need them to know your name
   before renewal.
3. **End-user advocate** — a power user who can speak to product value independently of the
   champion. This is your insurance policy if the champion leaves.

**How to build the economic buyer relationship without it feeling like you're going around the
champion:**
- Ask the champion to introduce you in the context of a business review ("I'd love to share
  what we've built together with your VP before the year is out")
- Invite them to a customer advisory board or exec briefing
- Reference them in your QBR deck by name — champions like to show internal wins to their bosses

**When to escalate multi-threading:** If you have only one contact in an account doing $100K+
ARR, that account is at risk regardless of what the health score says. Escalate to your AE or
founder to open a second relationship within 30 days.

## Decision Trees

### Should I open an expansion conversation now?

```
Has the account been live for at least 90 days?
├── No → Wait. Expanding before value is proven accelerates churn, not revenue.
└── Yes ↓

Has the account hit its original success metric?
├── No → Fix adoption before opening expansion. Use enterprise-onboarding skill.
└── Yes ↓

Have you observed a Tier 1 or Tier 2 trigger in the last 30 days?
├── No → Set a trigger monitoring cadence. Don't force a conversation without a hook.
└── Yes ↓

Do you have a relationship with someone other than the original champion?
├── No → Multi-thread first. A single-threaded expansion conversation creates dependency risk.
└── Yes → Open the expansion conversation using the Expansion Conversation Sequence.
```

---

### Expansion vs. upsell vs. cross-sell — who owns it?

```
Is the motion selling more of the same product (more seats, more volume)?
├── Yes → This is upsell. CS owns the conversation; AE owns the close if ACV > $50K.
└── No ↓

Is the motion selling a different product to the same buyer?
├── Yes → This is cross-sell. AE owns it. CS provides the intro and the warm handoff.
└── No ↓

Is the motion selling the same or different product to a new team or business unit?
└── Yes → This is expansion. AE owns it. Requires new champion identification and a new
          success metric — treat it like a new deal with a trust advantage, not a renewal.
```

---

### Is this a renewal negotiation or a pre-closed expansion?

```
Is the renewal date more than 90 days away?
├── Yes → You have time to expand before renewal. Do not conflate the conversations.
└── No ↓

Do you have verbal commitment from the economic buyer on any expansion?
├── No → You are in a renewal negotiation, not an expansion. Protect existing ARR first.
│        Do not offer discounts before confirming intent to renew at current scope.
└── Yes → Close the expansion first, then process the renewal. Combined paperwork is fine;
          the commercial conversation is not — expansion pricing should be set before
          renewal discounts are on the table.
```

## The Patterns That Look Reasonable But Kill Accounts

**"We'll expand at renewal."**
Renewal is the worst time to sell expansion. Procurement is involved. Budget is already
allocated. The customer is evaluating alternatives by definition. Expansion that closes at
renewal means you left 6–9 months of expansion ARR on the table.

**Conflating happiness with health.**
A customer who responds quickly to your emails and gives you 10/10 NPS is not necessarily
a customer who will expand or renew. Health is measured in product behavior, not sentiment.

**Expanding before the original use case delivers.**
The fastest path to churn is opening an expansion conversation before the customer has
realized value from what they already bought. It reads as a cash grab. The rule: no
expansion conversation until the original success metric is hit or formally revised.

**Letting the champion run your internal sale.**
Your champion is not a salesperson. They don't know how to navigate procurement, how to
build an ROI case for a CFO, or how to handle objections from a skeptical VP. If you hand
off the expansion to the champion without giving them the materials to close it, you're
betting the deal on their persuasion skills. Arm them.

**Discounting to close expansion.**
Expansion pricing should be the same or higher than the original contract on a per-unit
basis. If you discount to expand, you've told the customer that your pricing is negotiable
and they'll expect the same at renewal. Land-and-expand only works if expand is priced at
full value.

## The Story That Cost $800K in ARR

A $200K/year account was twelve months in. Champion loved the product. Usage was strong.
No trigger monitoring in place — the team ran quarterly business reviews on a calendar, not
on signals.

In month 11, the champion got promoted. The new owner of the relationship had a competing
vendor from a prior company. Renewal came up at month 12 with no pre-close, no second
contact in the account, and no expansion in flight. Procurement opened a competitive
evaluation. Lost at renewal to a vendor they'd used before.

The signals were there: the champion's promotion was a Tier 2 trigger. A new stakeholder
had started using the product three months prior — they never got introduced. No one ran
the multi-threading check. By the time the team realized what was happening, they were
already in procurement and out of leverage.

What would have changed it: a trigger-based monitoring process and a standing policy that
any account $100K+ ARR with a single contact triggers an exec relationship-building call
within 30 days.

## Quick Reference Checklists

### Monthly account health check (CS)
- [ ] Seat utilization rate pulled and trended
- [ ] Feature adoption breadth reviewed (are they using more or fewer features than last month?)
- [ ] Original success metric status confirmed or updated
- [ ] Contact map reviewed — do we have champion, economic buyer, and end-user advocate?
- [ ] Any Tier 1 or Tier 2 triggers observed? If yes, flag to AE within 48 hours.

### Pre-expansion conversation prep (AE)
- [ ] Account has been live 90+ days
- [ ] Original success metric confirmed delivered (not assumed)
- [ ] Trigger identified and documented
- [ ] Champion briefed on conversation framing before the call
- [ ] One-page outcome summary prepared and sent 48 hours in advance
- [ ] Economic buyer identified (even if not in the conversation yet)

### 90-days-to-renewal checklist (AE + CS)
- [ ] Health score ≥ 80? If not, focus on retention before expansion.
- [ ] Expansion conversation already had? If not, it's now or never.
- [ ] Verbal commitment from economic buyer on renewal scope?
- [ ] Multi-threaded? At least 2 contacts confirmed aware of renewal.
- [ ] Any open support issues or product gaps that could be used as leverage in negotiation?
- [ ] Renewal paperwork initiated internally (legal, finance) with 60-day lead time.
